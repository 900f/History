Exam Technique

Answer Structures

Step-by-step sentence frameworks for every question type on your GCSE History paper. Learn exactly what to include, how many points to make, and how to hit every mark.

2-Mark Question
~3–4 minutes 2–3 sentences Knowledge recall

What Is This Question?

Usually asks you to identify, describe, or briefly explain one thing. Often phrased as "Give one reason…", "Describe one feature of…", or "What does Source A suggest about…". No analysis needed — this is pure recall.

What You Need

  • 1 clear, accurate point — state the fact or feature directly
  • 1 brief supporting detail — add a date, name, or example to secure both marks
  • No introduction needed. No conclusion needed. Just the point and its evidence.
Sentence Framework

Formula: Point → Supporting detail

One reason was [POINT].
For example, [SUPPORTING DETAIL / DATE / NAME].

Example (Cold War):
One reason tensions increased was the Soviet blockade of Berlin. In June 1948, Stalin cut off all road, rail and canal routes into West Berlin, forcing the Western Allies to supply the city entirely by air.

Example (Elizabethan):
One feature of the Religious Settlement was the Act of Supremacy. This made Elizabeth "Supreme Governor" of the Church of England, a deliberately moderate title designed to appeal to Catholics as well as Protestants.

Examiner Tips

Don't waffle — examiners award marks quickly on short questions. If you write more than 3 sentences you're wasting time that should go on your 8, 16, or 24-mark answers. Always include a specific detail (date, person, place) to guarantee both marks rather than just one.

4-Mark Question
~5–7 minutes 4–6 sentences Describe / Explain briefly

What Is This Question?

Usually asks you to "Describe two…" or "Explain two reasons why…". You need two separate, developed points. Each point earns up to 2 marks: 1 for the point itself, 1 for the supporting detail or explanation.

What You Need

  • 2 separate points — clearly distinct, not the same idea repeated differently
  • Development for each — a date, statistic, name, consequence, or example for each point
  • No introduction or conclusion — launch straight into Point 1, then Point 2
  • Linking words like "Furthermore" or "Another reason" help the examiner see your two points are separate
Sentence Framework

Formula: Point 1 → Development 1 → Point 2 → Development 2

One reason was [POINT 1].
[DEVELOPMENT — date / name / statistic / example].

Another reason was [POINT 2].
[DEVELOPMENT — date / name / statistic / example].

Example (Cold War — "Describe two features of the Berlin Blockade"):
One feature was that Stalin cut off all land routes into West Berlin. In June 1948 he blocked all road, rail and canal access, intending to starve out the Western Allies.

Another feature was the Western response — the Berlin Airlift. Rather than abandon the city, Britain and the USA flew in supplies for over a year, eventually delivering up to 8,000 tonnes per day.

Examiner Tips

The most common mistake is making two points that are really the same point. Check: could your second point have been a development of your first? If yes, make them genuinely different. Each point must stand alone and earn its own 2 marks.

8-Mark Question
~10–12 minutes 3–4 paragraphs Explain / Analyse causes or consequences

What Is This Question?

Usually "Explain why…" — asking for multiple reasons or causes. You need at least three developed points, each in its own paragraph. Marks are awarded for knowledge, accurate detail, and explanation of how each point links to the question. No need for a balanced argument — just explain, explain, explain.

What You Need

  • Brief intro (1 sentence, optional) — signals to the examiner what your points will be
  • 3 paragraphs — each with a clear reason, specific evidence, and explanation of why this answers the question
  • PEEL structure per paragraph — Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link (back to the question)
  • Specific dates, names, statistics — these are the difference between Level 2 and Level 3 marks
Sentence Framework (PEEEEEEL)

Formula: Point → Evidence → Explanation → Explanation → Explanation → Explanation → Link

[INTRO — optional] There were several reasons why…

Paragraph 1
P: One reason [TOPIC] was [POINT].
E: For example, [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE — date, name, statistic].
E: This caused [EXPLANATION 1].
E: Furthermore, this meant that [EXPLANATION 2].
E: As a result, [EXPLANATION 3].
L: This links to the question because [DIRECT LINK BACK TO THE QUESTION WORDING].

Paragraph 2
P: Furthermore / Another reason was [POINT].
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE].
E / E / E: [Chain of explanation building on each other].
L: This therefore links to [question] because [LINK].

Paragraph 3
P: Finally, [POINT].
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE].
E / E / E: [Chain of explanation].
L: [Link back to question].

Example full PEEEEEEL paragraph (Cold War — "Explain why the Berlin Wall was built"):

P: One reason the Berlin Wall was built was the mass emigration of East Germans to the West.
E: Between 1945 and 1961, approximately 3.5 million people — nearly 20% of East Germany's population — fled through the open border in Berlin.
E: Many of those leaving were skilled workers, doctors, and engineers, causing a severe economic crisis in East Germany.
E: This meant East Germany could not function properly as a state, as it was losing the very people needed to run its economy and public services.
E: Ulbricht therefore pressured Khrushchev, arguing the wall was the only solution to prevent total collapse.
L: This directly links to why the wall was built — without stopping emigration, the communist state of East Germany faced economic and political collapse, making construction unavoidable.

Examiner Tips

The key to Level 3 (7–8 marks) is specific evidence and a strong Link sentence that explicitly ties your paragraph back to the question wording. Don't just describe — always explain why that point answers the question. The chain of explanations (E → E → E) is what separates a top-band answer from a mid-band one — each E should build on the last, not repeat it. Three solid PEEEEEEL paragraphs will comfortably hit 7–8 marks.

16-Mark Question
~20–25 minutes 5–6 paragraphs "How far do you agree…" / Evaluation

What Is This Question?

Usually phrased as "How far do you agree that [statement]?" This is a balanced argument question — you must argue on both sides and reach a supported judgement. You're being assessed on: knowledge and understanding, explanation, analysis, and the quality of your overall judgement. A one-sided answer is capped at the lower levels.

What You Need

  • Introduction — briefly signal your overall argument (agree / partially agree / disagree)
  • 2–3 paragraphs AGREEING with the statement — each using PEEL: Point, Evidence, Explanation, Link
  • 2 paragraphs DISAGREEING (counter-argument) — the alternative factors, also using PEEL
  • Conclusion — a clear overall judgement explaining which side is more convincing and why
  • Throughout: specific dates, names, statistics, and your L sentence explicitly tying each paragraph back to the question
Sentence Framework (PEEL throughout)

INTRO: I [agree / largely agree / partially agree] that [restate statement]. This is because [brief reason 1] and [brief reason 2], although [counter-argument].

AGREE Para 1
P: One reason to agree is [POINT].
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE — date, name, statistic].
E: This shows that [EXPLANATION LINKED TO STATEMENT].
L: This therefore supports the view that [LINK BACK TO THE EXACT QUESTION WORDING].

AGREE Para 2
P: Furthermore, [POINT].
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE].
E: This [EXPLANATION].
L: This links to [question] because [LINK].

AGREE Para 3 (optional)
P: In addition, [POINT]. E / E / L…

COUNTER Para 1
P: However, there are reasons to disagree. [ALTERNATIVE POINT].
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE].
E: This suggests that [EXPLANATION].
L: This challenges the statement because [LINK — how this factor undermines the original claim].

COUNTER Para 2
P: Furthermore, [ALTERNATIVE POINT]. E / E / L…

CONCLUSION: Overall, I [agree / disagree / partially agree] because [MAIN REASON — your strongest point]. While [ACKNOWLEDGE COUNTER], [YOUR JUDGMENT — why your side outweighs the other]. The most important factor was [MOST SIGNIFICANT POINT] because [WHY IT MATTERS MOST].

Example PEEL paragraph ("How far do you agree that the Soviet Union was responsible for the Cold War?"):

P: One reason to agree is that Soviet expansion into Eastern Europe directly threatened Western security.
E: By 1948, Stalin had imposed communist governments on Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania and Bulgaria — the "Iron Curtain" described by Churchill in 1946.
E: This showed that the USSR was aggressively expanding its sphere of influence rather than cooperating with its wartime allies.
L: This therefore supports the view that the Soviet Union was primarily responsible, as it was Soviet actions — not American ones — that first broke the post-war alliance and created the conditions for Cold War tension.

The Level Descriptors

Level 1 (1–4): Simple statements, limited knowledge, no real argument.
Level 2 (5–8): Some explanation, some evidence, but one-sided or undeveloped.
Level 3 (9–12): Explains both sides, uses specific evidence, begins to reach a judgement.
Level 4 (13–16): Analytical, well-evidenced on both sides, clear and convincing overall judgement explaining why one factor outweighs the other.

Examiner Tips

The conclusion is where Level 4 is won or lost. Don't just say "Overall I agree." Say why your argument is stronger — what makes it the most convincing interpretation? Compare the two sides directly: "While X was important, Y was more significant because…". This explicit comparative judgement is what separates top-band from mid-band answers.

24-Mark Question (+4 SPaG)
~35–40 minutes 6–8 paragraphs Extended essay / Significance / Change & Continuity

What Is This Question?

The biggest question on the paper — a full essay worth 24 marks for content plus 4 additional marks for SPaG (Spelling, Punctuation and Grammar). Usually phrased as "[Statement]. How far do you agree?" or "[Factor] was the main reason for [event]. How far do you agree?" You are expected to write a fully structured, balanced, analytical essay demonstrating the highest level of historical thinking.

What You Need

  • Introduction — clear thesis stating your overall argument, the factors you'll discuss, and a brief counter-argument signpost
  • 3 paragraphs SUPPORTING the statement — each with point, specific evidence, explanation, and explicit link to the question
  • 2–3 paragraphs CHALLENGING the statement — developed alternative factors with the same level of evidence and explanation
  • Conclusion — a fully reasoned judgement that compares the relative importance of factors, not just a summary
  • SPaG (4 marks) — clear sentences, accurate spelling, correct punctuation, varied vocabulary; avoid bullet points in essay
Full Essay Framework

INTRODUCTION (3–4 sentences):
I [agree / largely agree / disagree] that [restate the statement]. [Factor in statement] was significant because [one-sentence reason]. However, [alternative factor 1] and [alternative factor 2] were also important in [context]. Overall, I believe [your main argument] was the most significant factor because [headline reason].

PARAGRAPH 1 — Main factor (agrees with statement):
P: [Statement factor] was important because [POINT].
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE — date, name, statistic].
E: This demonstrates that [EXPLANATION — how it answers the question].
L: This therefore supports the argument that [DIRECT LINK BACK TO QUESTION WORDING].

PARAGRAPH 2 — Second point agreeing:
P: Furthermore, [POINT].
E: [EVIDENCE]. E: [EXPLANATION]. L: This links to [question] because [LINK].

PARAGRAPH 3 — Third point agreeing:
P: In addition, [POINT]. E / E / L…

PARAGRAPH 4 — Counter-argument (challenges statement):
P: However, there is evidence that [ALTERNATIVE FACTOR] was equally / more important.
E: [SPECIFIC EVIDENCE].
E: This suggests that [EXPLANATION — how this factor also caused the outcome].
L: This challenges the statement because without [ALTERNATIVE FACTOR], [outcome] might not have happened in the same way.

PARAGRAPH 5 — Second counter-argument:
P: Additionally, [ANOTHER ALTERNATIVE FACTOR]. E / E / L…

PARAGRAPH 6 (optional — synthesis):
P/L: [FACTOR A] and [FACTOR B] were connected because… — showing how factors interlink earns the highest marks.

CONCLUSION (4–6 sentences):
Overall, I [agree / disagree / partially agree] that [restate statement]. [Main supporting argument — why factor in question was significant]. However, [best counter-argument]. The most important factor was [YOUR CHOICE] because [WHY IT OUTWEIGHS THE REST — consider scale, timing, or consequence]. While [other factors] played a role, [your chosen factor] was ultimately decisive because [final reason].

SPaG — 4 Bonus Marks
  • Spelling: Spell key terms correctly — "Khrushchev", "containment", "propaganda", "excommunication". Practise the hard ones.
  • Punctuation: Use full stops, commas, and capital letters correctly. Avoid run-on sentences.
  • Grammar: Write in full sentences. No bullet points. Keep tenses consistent (past tense throughout).
  • Vocabulary: Use precise historical language — don't just write "things got worse"; write "tensions escalated", "relations deteriorated", "the crisis intensified".
  • 4 SPaG marks breakdown: 4 = high level of accuracy and varied vocabulary; 3 = generally accurate with some errors; 1–2 = frequent errors that obscure meaning
The Level Descriptors

Level 1 (1–6): Simple narrative, limited knowledge, no real argument or structure.
Level 2 (7–12): Some explanation, some evidence, but one-sided or lacks developed analysis.
Level 3 (13–18): Balanced — addresses both sides, uses specific evidence, attempts an overall judgement.
Level 4 (19–24): Analytical throughout, both sides fully developed, convincing judgement comparing the relative weight of factors. The best answers explain why one factor is more significant than the others — not just what each factor was.

Top Exam Tips

Plan before you write — spend 2–3 minutes jotting down your 3 agreeing points and 2–3 counter points. This stops you going blank mid-essay.

Specific evidence is everything — vague answers stay at Level 2. "The Berlin Wall was built in 1961 to stop 3.5 million East Germans from fleeing West" is far stronger than "the wall was built to stop people escaping."

The conclusion is where Level 4 is won — explicitly compare your strongest argument with your best counter-argument and explain why yours wins. Use the phrase "the most significant factor was… because…" to signal top-band thinking.

Time management — 35–40 minutes max. If you run out of time, write your conclusion first before finishing all your paragraphs.
